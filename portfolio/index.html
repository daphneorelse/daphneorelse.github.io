Roadmap

1. Background Research

In choosing a problem space such as music education for attention-disordered students, our literature review indicated game-ification and social networking were promising angles with which to design a solution.

2. Needfinding

Talking to several ADHD musicians taught us more about existing tools and habits they use to deepen and lengthen their music learning.

3. Solution Ideation

By building several POVs of our needfinding subjects, we considered a wide solution space including matchmaking systems, productivity software, and RPG games.

4. Experience Prototypes

Based on our early-stage proposed solution, we formulated several experiments with ADHD musicians testing questions we have about our solution space:

  • How long do they like spending to learn music?
  • How easily can they achieve a "flow state"?
  • Are they affected by "choice paralysis" when practicing music?

5. Low-Fi Prototype

Upon testing our assumptions, we chose Melody Meadows for its promise in achieving user flow states and helping users become invested in their learning. Our first prototype focused on three tasks flows:

  1. Learning a technique
  2. Playing a song
  3. Playing music with a friend

Observing new subjects using this prototype showed us they became invested in the characters, but wanted more customizability in their lessons.

All of my artistic sensibilities come from music. My love of mixing, sound design, and world-building is what drives all my work, no matter the medium.

Björk — Vulnicura

As easy as it is to call her my favorite musician, Björk will always truly be my favorite experience designer. Her holistic approach to art covers nearly everything—DIY music composition, cutting-edge live music productions, considered graphic design, and emotional clarity, all distilled into heartbreaking perfection on her eighth project, Vulnicura.

Aphex Twin — Drukqs

Not only is this very possibly my most played album of all time, what I love most is the story behind it's release. James had no intent to release any of the music on this project, but only after mistakenly leaving a MP3 player on a plane with all these songs did he consider a release, only to beat any potential leaks. The music is kinetic, haunting, often exhilerating, and reminds me to just put my work out there, regardless of whether I think it's good enough or not.

Though it's the medium I know the least about vis-à-vis technique, I experience fashion just as viscerally as thunderous music or heart-wrenching film. What I appreciate about this kind of world-building is that, ultimately, it must reside on the human body, and coming to terms with this constraint is what allows for genius design.

Alexander McQueen — Horn of Plenty FW09

What makes McQueen my favorite designer is truly his love and appreciation of women, and his masterful tailoring which always showed reverence of the human form beneath. This 2009 collection, dubbed Horn of Plenty: Everything But the Kitchen Sink, commented upon the growing beast of fast fashion and the piles of trash that clothes were becoming. In these clothes I see the surface details, like houndstooth run amok, clown-like beauty, powerful white, black, and red—but above all, I see the women beneath. Armored, decorated, feathered, and transformed, wearing McQueen makes a woman mythical, terrifying, and somehow still human.

Courrèges — FW23

Nicolas di Felice, creative director of French legacy fashion house Courrèges since 2020, is my favorite living designer because of his ability to transform a brand. Courrèges as a house had seen its heyday in the 60s, when its futurist vision of fashion-to-come became pervasive, touting geometric cuts, surprising mini-skirts, and space-age design philosophy. Tasked with bringing a retro-futurist vision literally into the future, Felice excels. He embraces vintage, gemotric cuts and transforms it for the modern woman, while still remaining forward-thinking. Fall/Winter 2023 features lighting from a phone screen, showing how Felice is always meditating on how technology shapes the way we see ourselves.
