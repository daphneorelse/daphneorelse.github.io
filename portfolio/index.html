Overview

2 week design sprint

Solo design project

On a lazy Saturday in January, the idea for a peer critique platform popped into my head—and off I went.

View the Full Design Process

1. User Research

Talking to several artists in disciplines such as creative writing, music, and visual art showed me the paramount importance of flexibility in how projects are hosted by the design and how users direct one another on what kind of critique they're looking for. After discussing things like the difference between in-person vs. digital critique, synchronous vs. asynchronous critique, prompt-sharing, trust-building, and the emotional rise & fall of critique, I was more convinced than ever a digital solution was promising.

2. POVs & Ideation

By building several POVs of my research participants and understanding where a product could meet their needs, I began making decisions about how my design would take form. I eventually landed on a desktop website solution for asynchronuous critique trading, bolstered by a mutual rating system and tuned recommendations to help users find other artists like themselves.

3. Architecture

Once the solution started to take form, I formalizd all my ideas by building a top-down sitemap and full user flow diagram showing the decision paths of seven unique user personas. This step is what fully solidified the project in my mind and got me excited to prototype.

4. Low-Fi Prototype

Using Figma, I began piecing together a rough wireframe of all my screens and flows. My biggest challenges here were formalizing the project creation input methods (deciding on tag searching was important) and making each page's hierachical structure as visible as possible.

5. Low-Fi Testing

User testing told me without a shadow of a doubt what wasn't working (putting the Learn tab in the user profile section was a clear mistake) and that the visual & conceptual differences between received and completed critiques were not distinguished enough. However, participants also made it clear I was on the right track in terms of my design for trustworthiness and artistic community.

6. UI Kit & Branding

The importance of building trust and giving honest critique led me to prioritize commanding authority with my design, but I also knew the design should not be distracting enough to take away from the artists' projects. These choices laid the foundation of my UI Kit and branding, which took inspiration from vintage manuscripts, wrought iron, and higher education and manifested as ornamental flourishes, inky blue & parchment tan, and literary serif typefaces.

7. Hi-Fi Prototype

In translating my low-fi prototype into more a vivid state, I found subtle ways of incorporating my newer, more stylish design elements while decluttering inessential noise. I incorporated feedback from Low-Fi testing, such as making a dedicated Learn tab, a section for received Crit ratings, and better hierarchical design of the user project page.

8. Usability Testing & Priority Revision

Testing confirmed my feeling that the Hi-Fi prototype was a huge improvement in usability over the Low-Fi, and artists really resonated with the amount of flexibility they're given in project creation and filling out critique forms. Some feedback prompted me to implement small but impactful changes that help users deliver their best critique possible, as well as increasing visibility of some lower-priority information to the home screen.

Roadmap

Overview

Design period of 5 months

Solo project

Goal: to design and build an industry-standard audio production plugin, hostable in many digital audio workstations and intuitive to use for a wide user-base

0. Needfinding

As an amateur music producer, finding a problem space in the audio plugin format was no issue. An idea I had for a new track necessitated a tool that would let me synthesize a series of explosion sounds, with an array of effects and fine controls that would let me build a dynamic composition. This problem implied a complex and challenging design and directly inspired my solution.

1. Background Research

To point me in the right direction of a solution, I first researched existing solutions to the problem. No audio plugin existed outright as a perfect fit, though I learned about what plugins other producers would use as part of a chain to achieve their desired sound. Additionally, I reviewed a wide array of state-of-the-art plugins to understand what kinds of design inspire me and what falls short.

2. Experience Prototype

My research prompted me to create an explosion synthesis chain with existing tools in my digital audio workstation—this chain consisted of two synthesis sources, four audio effects, and two modulation sources. I experimented with what sounds it could create and began to understand which controls were necessary & intuitive, and obsolete & obscure.

This prototype showed me I wanted much more control with the distortion unit, less control of the reverb unit, and a dual-envelope view of both synthesis sources so I could keep track of the plugin state at a glance.

3. Style Standards

In creating a tool for explosive sounds, I first gathered inspiration for UI elements from naval ship controls, depictions of nuclear bomb detonators, and traditional oscilloscopes. This directed my choice of color palette, which draws from these tools, measurement instruments, warning lights, vintage monitors, and hardware buttons.

4. UI Design

Over multiple iterations and several months, I designed vector assets and coded the complete interface with Juce/C++. UI components needed to balance readability, smooth control, flexibility, host parameter linking, and thematic consistency, and the overall layout needed to consider compactness, visual hierarchy, modularity (for reordering effects), and workflow.

5. Audio Processing Architecture

Alongside UI design and following the final interface design, I researched existing documentation of anti-aliased oscillator synthesis, noise synthesis, realtime automatible filter design, and appropriate distortion algorithms for my target sound. Eventually, I built six seperate processing classes that managed over 80 host parameters and performed thread-safe audio processing with state-of-the-art atomics management.

6. Testing

With the program running smoothly with a complete interface, I needed to rigorously test it in several host DAWs to ensure compatibility with a variety of parameter architectures, plugin file formats, and GUI hosting environments. I also put the program through a series of stress tests to ensure it could handle a high amount of simulatenous parameter updates and various sample rates.

7. Branding

Branding LaunchCodes came naturally from the designed controls and assets, which indicate a font and thematic style to adhere to, namely tactical, weapon-focused imagery with sharp angles. Creating a company image also followed suit, where Armory evoked the weapon-like products and daring nature of the product design.

8. Website Design

To allow users to explore its features and listen to sound demos, I created a website from scratch complete with animations, interactive assets, videos, and adhering to the style standards of the product itself.

Roadmap

Overview

10 week design period

Team of 4 designers

Goal: to design a high fidelity prototype of a digitial solution to a modern global challenge

1. Background Research

In choosing a problem space such as music education for attention-disordered students, our literature review indicated game-ification and social networking were promising angles with which to design a solution.

2. Needfinding

Talking to several ADHD musicians taught us more about existing tools and habits they use to deepen and lengthen their music learning.

3. Solution Ideation

By building several POVs of our needfinding subjects, we considered a wide solution space including matchmaking systems, productivity software, and RPG games.

4. Experience Prototypes

Based on our early-stage proposed solution, we formulated several experiments with ADHD musicians testing questions we have about our solution space:

  • How long do they like spending to learn music?
  • How easily can they achieve a "flow state"?
  • Are they affected by "choice paralysis" when practicing music?

5. Low-Fi Prototype

Upon testing our assumptions, we chose Melody Meadows for its promise in achieving user flow states and helping users become invested in their learning. Our first prototype focused on three tasks flows:

  1. Learning a technique
  2. Playing a song
  3. Playing music with a friend

Observing new subjects using this prototype showed us they became invested in the characters, but wanted more customizability in their lessons.

6. Med-Fi Prototype

Increasing fidelity for us meant building our previous taskflows according to our formalized style sheet in Figma, and fleshing out the characters' world with basic top-down 2D graphics. We also built out our tasks more elaborately, including designing for feedback from game characters while users are playing a song.

7. Usability Study

Testing our med-fi prototype with four target users showed us what was working (namely, dialogue boxes with several branching options), and what users wished for (e.g. more inuitive instrument and music UI, town navigation, and more substance in the playing with a friend taskflow).

8. Heuristic Evaluation

An evaluation of our med-fi prototype by ourselves and four other design students yielded a total of 68 violations, spanning across 12 different design standard categories. Some such violations indicated we should take inspiration from mobile game design in indicating certain character interactions, and to take inspiration from more static apps that have more consistent navigation icons.

9. Med-Fi Redesign

To address both the critiques voiced from usability study and the heuristic evaluation, we underwent a redesign focused on increasing the visibility of game state, standardizing navigation, improving readability, and building out a more extensive playing with a friend taskflow.

We tested this redesign with three more target users who indicated the need for a more distinct technique-learning taskflow, separate from song-playing.

10. Hi-Fi Prototype

Our final prototype for Melody Meadows polished user experience by focusing on visual consistency to make the in-game environments more cohesive. We designed a more distinct technique-learning taskflow and made navigation between tasks more simple with the use of a map.

11. Website

To showcase our design process and continue fleshing out our branding and design cohesion, we created a website outfitted with promotional motion graphics.

All of my artistic sensibilities come from music. As a childhood piano player-turned-composer and producer, the craft of a song will be a lifelong obsession of mine. And it goes beyond my own music—my love of mixing, sound design, and world-building is what drives all my work, no matter the medium.

Björk — Vulnicura

As easy as it is to call her my favorite musician, Björk will always truly be my favorite experience designer. Her holistic approach to art covers nearly everything—DIY music composition, cutting-edge live music productions, considered graphic design, and emotional clarity, all distilled into heartbreaking perfection on her eighth project, Vulnicura.

Aphex Twin — Drukqs

Not only is this very possibly my most played album of all time, what I love most is the story behind it's release. James had no intent to release any of the music on this project, but only after mistakenly leaving a MP3 player on a plane with all these songs did he consider a release, only to beat any potential leaks. The music is kinetic, haunting, often exhilerating, and reminds me to just put my work out there, regardless of whether I think it's good enough or not.

As a Tumblr user since tween-hood, the time I've spent over the course of my life looking at art and photography would likely shock me and you alike. Over the years I've discovered my prediliction for surrealism, vivid color, and humor, as well as creating it myself :)

Parker S. Jackson

I proudly say I've been a long-time fan of Parker S. Jackson because I just think what he does is so singular. His work has progressed over the years from more exploratory studies of the mundanely grotesque, to skillful surrealist paintings of vivid, nightmarish creatures, to now where he adopts properly classical techniques of painting to execute often funny, illogical, and always arresting tableaus of debauchery. Truly a modern-day Hieronymus Bosch, Jackson's work always inspires within me a childlike terror and wonder.

Jang Koal

Koal is a pop-surrealist artist born in South Korea that began her career as an illustrator. I think this background highly informs her current work, which often feels inspired by animation and cartoon in its reverance of subject character. The women she often depicts are stoic, pleasing, and somehow can portray both a sense of peace and mischief in me at once. I found her work online a few months ago and have since been completely captivated.

Though it's the medium I know the least about vis-à-vis technique, I experience fashion just as viscerally as thunderous music or heart-wrenching film. What I appreciate about this kind of world-building is that, ultimately, it must reside on the human body, and coming to terms with this constraint is what allows for genius design.

Alexander McQueen — Horn of Plenty FW09

What makes McQueen my favorite designer is truly his love and appreciation of women, and his masterful tailoring which always showed reverence of the human form beneath. This 2009 collection, dubbed Horn of Plenty: Everything But the Kitchen Sink, commented upon the growing beast of fast fashion and the piles of trash that clothes were becoming. In these clothes I see the surface details, like houndstooth run amok, clown-like beauty, powerful white, black, and red—but above all, I see the women beneath. Armored, decorated, feathered, and transformed, wearing McQueen makes a woman mythical, terrifying, and somehow still human.

Courrèges — FW23

Nicolas di Felice, creative director of French legacy fashion house Courrèges since 2020, is my favorite living designer because of his ability to transform a brand. Courrèges as a house had seen its heyday in the 60s, when its futurist vision of fashion-to-come became pervasive, touting geometric cuts, surprising mini-skirts, and space-age design philosophy. Tasked with bringing a retro-futurist vision literally into the future, Felice excels. He embraces vintage, gemotric cuts and transforms it for the modern woman, while still remaining forward-thinking. Fall/Winter 2023 features lighting from a phone screen, showing how Felice is always meditating on how technology shapes the way we see ourselves.

As my newest and deepest interest of the moment, martial arts have overwhelmed me of late with how much beauty and power they've added to my life. I respect all martial artists for their commitment in the face of personal risk, but I've come to love some disciplines most of all.

Nanquan

As distinguished from its longer-ranged northern counterpart Changquan, Nanquan is the modern southern Chinese martial arts discipline of barehanded fighting that saw widespread popularization in the 1960s. I became enamored with this style because of its signiture low stances and forceful vocalizations known as fasheng, a combination of which is staggering to witness even on a screen—this has remained my favorite of the wushu competitive events.

Knife Throwing

Power, precision, and deadliness all converge in knife throwing, which I just can't get enough of. Between western and eastern styles, I find myself more attracted to the artful and complicated movements eastern throwers pull off while nailing their knives into bullseyes. I've linked my favorite thrower above, a prodigous girl named Zhang Keyi who demonstrates so much commitment to her craft and inspires me to keep my own skills just as sharp.
